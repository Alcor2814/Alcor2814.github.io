Comets

Comets are leftovers from the formation of the solar system, orbiting the sun at varying distances ever since.
Comets are composed of dust, rock, and ices and range from a few miles to tens of miles wide.
There are likely billions of comets in the Kuiper Belt and the Oort Cloud, though the number of known comets is only 3,910.
The most famous comet is Halley's comet, a comet with a short-period orbit.
Halley's Comet becomes visible from Earth every 75-76 years, the only short-period comet that can appear twice in a human lifetime.
In 1986 Halley's Comet was studied up close by 8 scientific spacecraft from around the Earth.
Comets develop their characteristic tails from gases and dust emmitted as they heat up on proximity to the Sun.
Short-period comets are comets which take less than 200 years to orbit the sun, they reside in the juiper belt of icy bodies beyond Neptune.
The Oort Cloud is home to long-period comets, which take as long as 30 million years to complete one trip!
