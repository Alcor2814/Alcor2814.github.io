Stars

Stars are giant balls of hot gas, mostly composed of hydrogen and helium with small amounts of other elements.
Stars vary in scale, affecting their life cycle, luminosity, and the violence of their death.
All stars begin the same, with clumps of gas and matter gathering together due to gravity and heating up due to friction.
At first the majority of a protostar's energy comes from heat released by its initial collapse.
For the longest stretch of the star's life immense pressure and temperature will squeeze the hydrogen atoms together to form helium, a process called Nuclear Fusion.
Stars that are stably undergoing nuclear fusion are known as Main Sequence Stars, such as the Sun.
The violence of a star's death varies depending on the mass of the star.
A low-mass star will keep expanding until it becomes a sub-giant or giant star , with some giants becoming unstable over time and ejecting its atmosphere to form a nebula which leaves behind a slowly cooling white dwarf.
A high-mass star will continue to fuse its elements beyond carbon and into iron, buying itself increasingly less time until the star runs out of energy and collapses, releasing a supernova and leaving behind a neutron star or black hole.
