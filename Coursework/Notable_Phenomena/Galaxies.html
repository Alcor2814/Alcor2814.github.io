Galaxies

Galaxies are vast collections of stars, planets, and clouds of gas and dust bound together by gravity.
Galaxies can span from trillions of stars at millions lightyears across to a few thousand stars spanning a few hundred lightyears.
The specific shape of galaxies vary, usually spirals or ellipticals though they can form very irregularly.
Most galaxies are between 10 and 13.6 billion years, though others are still forming with the youngest known galaxy forming approximately 500 million years ago.
Gravity makes galaxies further organise into groups known as clusters, which may themselves be arranged into superclusters which are not gravitationallly bound.
The Milky Way sits in a cluster known as the Local Group, itself a part of the Virgo Supercluster which is itself part of the Laniakea Supercluster.
The Milky Way is Earth's Galaxy, a spiral Galaxy spanning more than 100,000 light years.
It takes the solar system approximately 240 million years to orbit the Milky Way once.
The Milky Way is approximately 2.5 milion lightyears away from the Andromeda Galaxy.
