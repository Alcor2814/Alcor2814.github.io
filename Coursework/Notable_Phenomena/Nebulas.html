Nebulas

A nebula is a giant cloud of dust and gas, sometimes hundreds of lightyears in diamete.
Nebulae are often called stellar nurseries due to the fact their gas, dust, and other material will clump together and eventually form stars.
Nebulae also give rise to planets with the remaining material left over from stellar formation.
Though the first nebulae were observed as early as 964 they were not particularly studied until the 17th Century with advancements in telescopes.
In 1715 Edmond Halley, the astronomer Halley's Comet was named after, published a list of six nebulae which kicked off increased interest in discovering nebulae.
The supernova which formed the Crab Nebula was observed by Arabic and Chinese Astronomers in 1054.
Nebulae typically form as a result of supernova explosions, massive explosions of material flung off from stars who have reached the end of their life cycles.
Some nebulae do form from gas that hasn't yet been claimed by a nebula clumping together.
Other nebulae may be formed by stars shedding material for various reasons.
