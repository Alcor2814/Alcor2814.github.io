JUPITER

Most Massive:
Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and more than twice as massive as all other planets in the solar system combined.
Jupiter is 11 times wider than Earth at 69,911 kilometres radius.
Jupiter sits at an average distance of 778 million kilometres from the sun, 5.2 Astronomical Units.

Classical History:
Jupiter was formally given its name in 1976, after the king of the Roman pantheon.
Both the ancient Greek and Roman civilisations named the planet after their king gods, Zeus and Jupiter.
Jupiter is believed to be the oldest planet in the solar system, forming one million years after the sun and 50 million before the Earth.

Orbit:
Jupiter is home to four large moons and 91 recognised smaller moons, with Europa's oceans being a potential source of life!
Jupiter has rings composed of small, dark particles which were discovered by Voyager 1 in 1979.
Jupiter completes one orbit of the sun every 4,333 Earth days, its rotations are much faster with one day taking 10 hours.

Atmosphere:
As a gas giant Jupiter is entirely atmosphere, as far as can be observed.
The specific structure of Jupiter is theoretical but it likely has three layers of ammonia ice, ammonium hydrosulfide crystals, and water ice and vapor.
Due to its lack of a surface Jupiter is rife with winds reaching as fast as 539 km/h and storms lasting as long as 300 years, like the Great Red Spot.
