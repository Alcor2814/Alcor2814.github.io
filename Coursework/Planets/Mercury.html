MERCURY

Tiny Rock:
Mercury is only slightly larger than Earth's moon, making it the smallest planet in the solar system. Mercury orbits the sun at an average distance of 58 million kilometres, approximately 1/3 of an AU. Mercury has an eccentric, egg-shaped orbit taking it from a low of 47 million kilometres from the sun to a high of 70 million kilometres in a round trip of 88 days. At this distance it takes sunlight 3.2 minutes to travel from the Sun to Mercury on average.

Formation:
Mercury formed about 4.5 billion years ago when gravity pulled gas and dust together until a planet formed.
Mercury is a terrestrial planet, indicated by its core, rocky mantle, and solid crust. Mercury is frequently bombarded by asteroids giving it its signature pockmarked surface.

Atmosphere and Magnetosphere:
Mercury lacks an actual atmosphere, instead it possess a thin exosphere made up of atoms blasted off the surface by solar wind and meteroids.
The exosphere is comprised mostly of oxygen, sodium, hydrogen, helium, and potassium.
Mercury's magnetosphere is 1% as strong as Earth's but can still produce magnetic tornadoes through interaction with solar wind.

Surface:
Most of Mercury's surface appears greyish-brown to the human eye, with bright streaks called crater rays formed from asteroid/comet impacts.
Temperatures on mercury operate over an extreme range, from 430 degrees Celsius to -180 degrees Celsius across the course of its 59 Earth days rotation.
