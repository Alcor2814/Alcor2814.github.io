SATURN

Sixth from the Sun:
Saturn is a gas giant similar to Jupiter, each made mostly of hydrogen and helium.
Saturn is also the farthest planet from Earth discovered by the unaided human eye, and is 9 times wider than Earth at 120,500 kilometres diameter.
Saturn was named for the Roman god of agriculture and wealth.
Saturn has 146 officially recognised moons in orbit, with further awaiting official confirmation and official naming.

Rings:
Saturn's rings are believed to be pieces of comets, asteroids, or shattered moons which were torn apart by Saturn's gravity.
Ring particles range from dust-sized icy graisn to chunks as big as a house, some even as large as a mountain.
Saturn's rings extend up to 282,000 kilometres from the planet though vertical height is usually only up to 10 metres.
There are three main rings with four more recently discovered fainter rings.

Structure:
Saturn has an average density lower than water, meaning it could float in a container of water.
Winds in the upper atmosphere reach speeds of 500 metres per second.
Saturn's north-pole has a hexagon-shaped jet stream spanning about 30,000 kilometres forming a massive storm at its centre.

Orbit:
Saturn sits at an average distance of 1.4 billion kilometres from the sun, or 9.5 astronomical units. Saturn makes a complete orbit of the sun in 29.4 Earth years.
A day on Saturn takes only 10.7 hours.
Saturn has a 26.73 degree tilt relative to its orbit, giving it seasons much like Earth.
